# Contract Clause Reference Library

## Non-Compete Clauses

### What's Enforceable (UK)
A restrictive covenant is only enforceable if it:
1. Protects a **legitimate business interest** (trade secrets, customer relationships)
2. Is **no wider than reasonably necessary** to protect that interest
3. Is **reasonable** in duration, geography, and scope

**Reasonable parameters (UK employment):**
- Duration: 3–12 months (longer is increasingly hard to enforce)
- Geography: Where you actually worked/had client relationships (not "worldwide")
- Scope: Your actual role (not "entire industry")

**Red flag patterns:**
- "12 months, worldwide, any competing business" → Almost certainly unenforceable but still a threat
- Non-compete in a junior/low-skill role → Rarely has legitimate interest to protect
- Post-termination non-compete with no payment → May be unenforceable in some jurisdictions

**Negotiation ask:** "Limit the non-compete to [6 months], [relevant geographic area], and [specific competitive activities relevant to my role]."

## IP Assignment Clauses

### What's Fair vs What's a Grab
**Fair:** Employer owns work created in the course of employment, using company resources, for company purpose
**Overreach:** "All work created during employment, whether or not related to your duties, whether using company resources or not"

**Key battleground:** Side projects
- If you're a software developer, employer may legitimately claim software you write at home
- Must carve out: "excluding work unrelated to employer's business, created outside working hours, using personal resources"

**Pre-existing IP:** Always add a clause listing pre-existing IP you're bringing: "Schedule A — Pre-existing IP" and list it

**UK law:** Section 39 Patents Act 1977 — patents belonging to employee if not in course of normal duties. Copyright: employer owns work created in course of employment (CDPA 1988 s11)

## Termination Clauses

### Notice Periods
**Statutory minimum (UK):** 1 week per year of service (up to 12 weeks after 12 years)
**What to check:** Is contractual notice the same? Which governs on termination?

### Garden Leave
**What it is:** Employer keeps you on payroll but requires you to stay home during notice period
**Legitimate use:** Protecting confidential information, client relationships
**Red flag:** Unpaid garden leave — if you're on garden leave, you must be paid

### Summary Dismissal (Without Notice)
Only valid for gross misconduct. Examples: theft, violence, serious breach of confidence
**Red flag:** Contract lists minor infractions as "gross misconduct" — negotiate removal or narrowing

### PILON (Payment in Lieu of Notice)
Employer's right to pay you out rather than work your notice. Check: does it include bonuses, benefits? Often it shouldn't exclude these.

## Bonus & Commission Clauses

### Red Flags
- "Bonus is entirely at employer's discretion" — valid but means it can be zero and you can't challenge it
- "Must be employed at payment date" — means you lose bonus if terminated before it's paid, even if earned
- Clawback: "Bonus repayable if you leave within 12 months" — check the trigger and amount

### Negotiation
- Push for: "Bonus is formulaic, not purely discretionary, based on [specific metrics]"
- Add: "Pro-rated bonus payable on termination without cause"

## Liability Limitation Clauses

### In Commercial Contracts
**Common patterns:**
- Liability cap: "Our total liability shall not exceed the fees paid in the last 3 months" → May be adequate or completely inadequate depending on context
- Consequential loss exclusion: "We exclude all indirect and consequential loss" → Means they don't pay for lost profits caused by their failure
- Both combined: You could have a catastrophic failure and recover almost nothing

**Negotiation:**
- For SaaS/software: Push for liability cap = at least 12 months' fees
- For critical infrastructure: Request carve-out for data breaches, wilful misconduct, fraud
- Standard carve-outs that should NOT be capped: death/personal injury, fraud, fraudulent misrepresentation

## Dispute Resolution Clauses

### Arbitration
**What it means:** Disputes go to private arbitration, not courts
**Red flags:**
- Mandatory arbitration (waiving right to sue) in consumer contracts → Often unfair and may be unenforceable (UK CRA 2015)
- Class action waiver combined with arbitration → Eliminates collective redress
- Arbitrator selection favours company

### Jurisdiction
- "Exclusive jurisdiction of the courts of [distant state/country]" in consumer contract → Red flag, may be unenforceable under UK/EU consumer law
- For B2B: reasonable to have a specific jurisdiction, but should be accessible to both parties

## NDA Specific Clauses

### Definition of Confidential Information
**Too broad:** "All information disclosed by Company, whether marked confidential or not"
**Reasonable:** "Information that is marked confidential, or that a reasonable person would understand to be confidential given the circumstances of disclosure"

**Must have exclusions:**
- Information already publicly known
- Information independently developed
- Information received from third party without restriction
- Information required to be disclosed by law (with notice to disclosing party)

### Duration
- Perpetual NDA → Standard for genuine trade secrets, acceptable in limited circumstances
- 2-5 years → Standard for general business information
- "Indefinitely" for employee on product roadmap information → Red flag

### Return/Destruction of Materials
Should include: obligation to return or certify destruction on request, and confirmation clause
